1.3 Truncation Error

Local truncation error

Since we have used a finite number or terms mmm in the Taylor expansion, the approximated solution will not be exact. But, can we get a better understanding of this error?

Let's look more carefully at the approximations that we did in the previous section. Assuming that we can obtain an exact solution and its derivatives at ti−1t_{i-1}ti−1​, the approximated exact value at tit_iti​ can be expressed as

The final scheme, equivalent to the Forward Euler method for second order ODEs, is defined by the following steps:

 
Forward Euler Method

  1. Get the value of the second time derivative from the equation of motion:

u¨i−1=1m(F(ti−1)−kui−1−cu˙i−1).\ddot{u}_{i-1}=\frac{1}{m}(F(t_{i-1})-ku_{i-1}-c\dot{u}_{i-1}).u¨i−1​=m1​(F(ti−1​)−kui−1​−cu˙i−1​).

ui=ui−1+Δtu˙i−1.u_i=u_{i-1} + \Delta t\dot{u}_{i-1}.ui​=ui−1​+Δtu˙i−1​.

Global truncation error

We have seen that the local truncation error of the Forward Euler method is second order, meaning that at every step iii for i=1,...,Ni=1,...,Ni=1,...,N we will introduce an error of O(Δt2)\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)O(Δt2). Therefore, after NNN time steps, the global truncation error will be

ϵuN∼NO(Δt2)=TΔtO(Δt2)∼O(Δt).\epsilon_{u_N}\sim N\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)=\frac{T}{\Delta t}\mathcal{O}(\Delta t^2)\sim\mathcal{O}(\Delta t).ϵuN​​∼NO(Δt2)=ΔtT​O(Δt2)∼O(Δt).

This leads to a first order convergence rate for the global truncation error of the Forward Euler method.
